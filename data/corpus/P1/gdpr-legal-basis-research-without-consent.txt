Summary

While consent is widely used in genomic research, the GDPR permits other Article 6 and Article 9 bases in defined circumstances. Member States may provide specific research exemptions under Article 89 and national health/research law.

Article 6 bases (non-special-category processing)

(e) Public task — public authority or task in public interest.
(f) Legitimate interests — rarely sole basis for large-scale genomic databases; requires balancing test and usually not for special category without Article 9 basis.

Article 9 bases without consent (selected)

(j) Scientific research — processing necessary for scientific or historical research or statistical purposes in accordance with Article 89(1), based on Union or Member State law, proportionate to aim, with respect for essence of right to data protection.
(i) Public interest in public health — for threats or ensuring high standards of health care/medicinal products, per Member State law.
(h) Health or social care — processing by or under responsibility of professional subject to professional secrecy.

National research exemptions

Many EU Member States adopt legislation allowing biobank and registry processing for research with ethics approval, transparency duties, and opt-out instead of opt-in for certain archival health data. Applicability depends on local law — RegBot flags for human review when consent is absent.

Safeguards under Article 89(1)

Pseudonymisation where possible; technical and organisational measures; data minimisation; ethics review; proportionality; conditions for international transfers still apply under Chapter V.

Relevant provisions: Articles 6, 9(2)(i)(j), Article 89, Recital 159.
